What is Color Vision Deficiency?

Color Vision Deficiency (CVD), commonly called "color blindness," is a condition where the eyes perceive colors differently than typical trichromatic vision. It is almost always inherited, carried on the X chromosome, which explains why it affects far more males than females.

The human eye contains three types of cone photoreceptors — L (red-sensitive), M (green-sensitive), and S (blue-sensitive). In CVD, one or more of these cone types is absent, reduced in quantity, or shifted in spectral sensitivity.

Most forms of CVD are not "seeing in black and white" — instead, certain colors appear similar or identical. A protanope sees red as dark gray or near-black. A deuteranope confuses reds and greens. This has significant implications for any color-coded interface.

CVD is not a disease — it is a variation in visual perception. But it does mean that color-only information cues (red = bad, green = good) completely fail for a substantial portion of your users.

The four primary types

Protanopia

~1.3% males · 0.02% females

L-cones absent. Reds appear dark or brownish. Red-green confusion is severe.

Deuteranopia

~5.0% males · 0.35% females

M-cones absent. Most common CVD. Greens appear brown, confused with reds.

Tritanopia

~0.01% all genders

S-cones absent. Very rare. Blues appear greenish, yellows pinkish or gray.

Achromatopsia

~0.003% all genders

Total color blindness. Only luminance differences are perceived. Extremely rare.

WCAG 2.1 Contrast Standards

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) define contrast ratios that text and interactive elements must meet to be legible to people with low vision or CVD.

Contrast ratio is calculated from the relative luminance of two colors. A ratio of 21:1 is black on white (maximum). A ratio of 1:1 is identical colors (invisible).

WCAG 2.1 Level AA is the legal minimum in many jurisdictions. Level AAA is the enhanced target for critical interfaces.

Large text = 18pt or 14pt bold. UI components need ≥ 3:1.

CVD-Safe Design Principles

Never use color as the only information channel. Always pair color with shape, pattern, text label, or icon. "Red = error" alone fails for 8% of males.

Test with simulation. Use the ColorSight Simulator to see how your design looks under each CVD type before shipping.

Use the Wong palette. This 8-color palette (2011) is optimized to remain distinguishable under all major CVD types and for typical trichromatic vision.

Maintain luminance contrast. Even if hue information is lost, sufficient luminance difference preserves readability across all CVD types.

Avoid red/green pairs for critical information — together they affect ~6.3% of males. Use blue/orange as an accessible alternative.
